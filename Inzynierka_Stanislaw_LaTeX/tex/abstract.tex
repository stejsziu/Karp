This thesis concerns the design and verification of a control system for an unmanned underwater vehicle. In the initial phase, the system is intended for a remotely operated vehicle (ROV), however, its adaptation to an autonomous underwater vehicle (AUV) is planned. The main engineering challenge undertaken in this work is the analytical derivation of a nonlinear mathematical model of the vehicle's dynamics in six degrees of freedom, taking into account the specific characteristics of the aquatic environment, including the phenomena of added mass and hydrodynamic damping. As part of the thesis, classical control structures (PID) will be applied, alongside algorithms that account for the individual dynamics of the object. Due to the strong attenuation of electromagnetic waves underwater and the lack of a GPS signal, the feedback loop will be based on data from inertial measurement units (IMU) and a pressure sensor. The control system software will be developed based on the ROS 2 environment and verified using the Gazebo simulator. The entire software architecture will be prepared for direct deployment on physical hardware, such as a Raspberry Pi.
